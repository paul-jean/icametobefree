**Audible/ACX require a 1-hour minimum.** Your book, read as-is, doesn't qualify for the single biggest audiobook retailer. This isn't a wall — it's the fork the whole plan turns on. There are two honest ways through it, and they aren't mutually exclusive:

## Track A — reaching an hour (recommended)

You don't pad. You add material that makes the audiobook *richer* than the print book, which is exactly what a short-collection audiobook needs to not feel thin to a paying listener. Options, roughly in order of how naturally they fit this collection:

1. **Spoken introduction** (~3–5 min) — why you wrote it, what grief/love/renewal meant here. The audiobook format rewards this intimacy; print can't do it.
2. **A reflection before or after each poem** (~2–4 min × 9) — the story behind the poem, the moment it came from. This is the single biggest lever: it roughly doubles or triples runtime *and* is the kind of bonus content that makes an audiobook worth a credit. For a personal collection this is genuinely additive, not filler.
3. **A second, unhurried reading** of a few key poems, or the title poem, as a closing.
4. **Author's note on the title / on "Gaman"** — the Japanese concept, the starling motif.

Items 1 + 2 alone comfortably clear an hour and produce something a reader can't get on the page. Retailers accept — even like — this structure for poetry.

## Recording setup (self-narration)

FriesenPress runs this **remotely**: you record from home while their producer directs and monitors in real time, then their team edits and masters. So your job is a good room and good raw audio; theirs is everything after.

**Gear** (their own recommendation — modest, reusable for readings/podcasts):

- **Mic:** Samson Q2U (USB/XLR dynamic, ~US$70). Dynamic mic rejects room noise — the right choice for an untreated home space.
- **Headphones:** Audio-Technica ATH-M20X (~US$50).
- Total gear: **~US$120**, and you keep it for the Twisted Poets open mic promo, podcast guest spots, etc.

**Room:** smallest, least echoey space you have. A walk-in closet full of clothes is genuinely ideal (soft surfaces kill reverb). Record when your street and building are quietest.

**Prep before the red light:**

- Read the whole book aloud, start to finish, at least once. Poetry that scans on the page can trip the tongue — you'll find the lines that need a breath.
- Mark your pacing: where you slow, where you let silence sit. In poetry the pauses *are* the performance.
- Draft the spoken intro and the per-poem reflections (Track A) as loose notes, not a script to read stiffly — spoken, not recited.
- Physical: rested, hydrated, warm tea on hand. Voices tire; short sessions beat marathons.

**Performance notes that matter for this book specifically:**

- These are grief-and-renewal poems — resist "poetry voice." Speak them the way you'd say them to one person who's hurting. FriesenPress's own coaching line: picture the single listener you wrote it for.
- A smile and posture are audible. So is rushing. Slower than feels natural on the page.
- Misspoke a line? Stop, pause, retake from the top of the line. Editing removes the seam. Never restart the whole poem.

The audiobook on Audible is a *product*. The reading on your site is the *mission*. Build both.

Because you'll own the masters outright, we can put a clean reading of each poem directly on icametobefree.xyz — a small play button on each poem, and on each `/q/<id>/` share page. What that unlocks:

- Zero length minimum, zero paywall — the constraints that fight sharing simply don't apply.
- I can build the player (lightweight, same static-site approach, audio hosted alongside the poems). Trivial once you have the files.

My lean for a book whose whole premise is "meant to be shared": full poems free on the site, and the *paid* audiobook is the richer Track-A version with your intros and reflections. People who love the free reading buy the deeper one.
